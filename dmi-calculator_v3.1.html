The math behind the calculation

To get the combined fuel economy in km/L, we must convert the consumed battery energy (kWh) into an equivalent amount of gasoline (litres) that the engine would have needed to burn to produce that exact amount of energy.

Gasoline has an energy density of approximately 8.9 kWh/L. We apply a modifier for the Research Octane Number (RON), where higher-octane fuel allows for slightly better knock resistance and thermal efficiency, and factor in the known peak thermal efficiency (η) of the specific BYD DM-i generation engine (e.g., 46.06% or 0.4606 for the 5th-gen system).

Step 1 — RON Multiplier (MR)

The RON Multiplier (MR) is calculated relative to a base of 95 RON. Higher-octane fuels receive a small efficiency bonus; lower-octane fuels incur a penalty.

MR  =  1 + (R − 95) × 0.002
Step 2 — Battery Equivalent Fuel (Feq)

The equivalent litres of gasoline the engine would have burned to produce the same energy as the battery (Eb), scaled by engine thermal efficiency η and the RON modifier MR.

Feq  =  Eb 8.9 × (η × MR)
Step 3 — Combined Fuel Economy (Ec)

Distance travelled divided by the sum of actual fuel used (Fv) and the battery equivalent fuel (Feq), giving a single km/L figure.

Ec  =  D Fv + Feq
Step 4 — Trip Cost

Total cost is fuel spend plus electricity spend. Dividing by distance gives the cost per kilometre.

Ctotal  =  (Fv × Cf)  +  (Eb × Ce)
